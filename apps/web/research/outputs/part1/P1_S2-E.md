# Part-level allowlists for CFR Tier 3 Titles Group 2

**Cedar needs 57 parts across these four CFR titles — not the full ~2,000+ parts they contain.** Titles 20, 49, 10, and 2 are overwhelmingly irrelevant to Florida independent medical practices, but each harbors focused clusters of parts that impose real compliance obligations. Title 49's hazardous materials regulations (Parts 171–178) affect every waste-generating practice; Title 10's NRC Chapter I contains 14 parts governing radioactive material use (though Florida's Agreement State status shifts primary authority to state rules); Title 20's SSA disability provisions (Parts 404, 416) and immigration labor certifications (Parts 655, 656) touch all 14 practice types; and Title 2's relevance is entirely conditional on whether a practice receives federal grants.

All ambiguous classifications below have been resolved into definitive RELEVANT or IRRELEVANT determinations. The bias is toward inclusion: false negatives are treated as costlier than false positives.

### Ambiguity resolutions for Title 20

**Part 10 (FECA medical benefits) → RELEVANT (Low).** Initially ambiguous because FECA primarily governs federal employee benefits, not provider regulation. Resolved as relevant because Subpart E directly sets requirements for physician selection, medical report format, and billing procedures that apply to any treating provider. FL's substantial federal workforce makes encounter plausible.

**Part 411 (Ticket to Work) → RELEVANT (Low).** Could be excluded as primarily a beneficiary program. Included because practices can serve as Employment Networks under the program and the medical evaluation provisions directly involve treating physicians.

**Part 418 (Medicare subsidies) → RELEVANT (Low).** Governs IRMAA and Part D subsidies administered by SSA. Included because it affects patient cost-sharing and coverage access, which practices need to understand for Medicare patient populations.

**Part 702 (Longshore Act medical care) → RELEVANT (Low).** Narrow population, but FL's extensive port and maritime industry creates a non-trivial probability that Cedar practices near coastal areas treat covered workers.

For Cedar, **only two regulatory clusters matter**: the Hazardous Materials Regulations (Parts 171–180) that govern medical waste shipping, and the DOT drug/alcohol testing framework (Parts 40 and 382) for practices employing CDL holders. Everything else — railroads, aviation security, motor vehicle safety, transit, pipelines, Coast Guard — is irrelevant.

### Ambiguity resolutions for Title 49

**Part 40 and Part 382 (DOT drug testing) → RELEVANT (Conditional).** Classified as relevant despite being conditional on CDL employment because: (1) practices operating mobile medical units, large transport vehicles, or patient shuttle services may employ CDL holders; (2) the compliance burden is severe if applicable and non-compliance penalties are criminal; (3) the false-negative cost of excluding these parts is high.

**Part 175 (Carriage by Aircraft) → RELEVANT (Low).** The practice's obligation as an offeror of biological specimens shipped by air exists independently of the air carrier's compliance. Functional medicine, regenerative medicine, and other practices routinely ship diagnostic specimens to reference labs.

**Part 105 (PHMSA Definitions) → RELEVANT (Low).** Provides definitional support for Parts 171–178. Without it, interpretation of key terms used in the core HMR parts is incomplete.

**Part 180 (Packaging Maintenance) → RELEVANT (Low).** While most practices use single-use containers, practices that use reusable sharps containers or large reusable packaging for medical waste transport must comply. The cost of exclusion exceeds the cost of inclusion.

### Key compliance obligations for medical waste

Every Cedar practice generating regulated medical waste faces five core obligations under these parts: **(1)** proper packaging per §173.197/§173.134; **(2)** shipping papers per §172.200–205; **(3)** marking and labeling per §172.300+ and §172.400+; **(4)** triennial hazmat employee training per §172.700–704; and **(5)** PHMSA registration per §107.601–620 (unless exception applies). The §173.134 private/contract carrier exception allows simplified OSHA-conforming packaging — most FL practices likely qualify for this exception.

## Title 10: Energy — 14 relevant parts, all in NRC Chapter I

Title 10 contains seven chapters spanning nuclear energy regulation, DOE operations, and nuclear waste oversight. **Only Chapter I (NRC) contains any relevant material**, and within it, the radiation safety and medical byproduct material cluster (Parts 19–37) is what matters. Chapters II, III, X, XIII, XVII, and XVIII (all DOE or nuclear waste entities) are entirely irrelevant to medical practices.

**Critical context: Florida became an NRC Agreement State in 1964.** The Florida Department of Health, Bureau of Radiation Control, exercises primary regulatory authority over byproduct material under Chapter 64E-5, F.A.C. Federal 10 CFR parts remain relevant as the compatibility template, and NRC retains direct jurisdiction over transportation (Part 71), physical security of high-activity sources (Part 37), and reciprocity situations (Part 150).

### Ambiguity resolutions for Title 10

**Part 2 (NRC Practice and Procedure) → RELEVANT (Low).** Procedural rules for NRC proceedings. Included because any practice that holds an NRC license (or operates under reciprocity) could be subject to NRC enforcement proceedings and would need to understand these procedures.

**Parts 31, 32, 33 (General/specific byproduct licensing) → RELEVANT (Low).** These are supporting parts to the Part 30/35 framework. Part 31 covers generally-licensed devices that may appear in medical settings; Part 32 covers radiopharmaceutical manufacturers (relevant to compounding pharmacies); Part 33 covers broad-scope licenses (unlikely but not impossible for large practices).

**Part 37 (Physical protection of high-activity sources) → RELEVANT (Low).** Most independent practices won't possess Category 1/2 quantities. However, NRC retains direct jurisdiction even in Agreement States, making this the one part where federal authority is unambiguously primary. Any practice with HDR brachytherapy or blood irradiators must comply.

**Parts 170 and 171 (NRC fees) → RELEVANT (Low).** Most FL practices pay state fees, not NRC fees. Included because reciprocity operations and any direct NRC licensing trigger these fee schedules.

### Florida Agreement State implications

**Most Cedar practices will never directly interact with 10 CFR.** Florida's Chapter 64E-5, F.A.C., is the primary compliance framework for radioactive material use. However, 10 CFR remains relevant in three scenarios: **(1)** Part 37 physical security requirements, where NRC retains direct jurisdiction; **(2)** Part 71 transportation requirements, which apply federally regardless of Agreement State status; and **(3)** reciprocity under Part 150 when a Florida-licensed practice operates in a non-Agreement State. Additionally, **NRC does not regulate x-ray machines, CT/DEXA scanners, fluoroscopy, lasers, ultrasound, or MRI** — these are governed by Florida state radiation rules. The practice types most likely to encounter NRC-regulated radioactive materials are Compounding Pharmacy (radiopharmaceuticals), Pain Management (brachytherapy), and Primary Care (nuclear medicine diagnostics).

## Title 2: Grants and Agreements — 19 relevant parts, all conditional on federal funding

Title 2 is structurally distinct from the other three titles. Its relevance is **entirely conditional** — it applies only if a practice receives or applies for federal financial assistance. Most independent medical practices in Cedar's 14 categories do not receive federal grants. However, practices participating in HRSA community health funding, NIH research, SAMHSA treatment grants, DOJ opioid response programs, or CMS innovation models face full compliance obligations under these parts.

**VA Parts 801/802 → RELEVANT (Low).** Initially borderline because VA community care is primarily a contract relationship, not a grant. **ONDCP Part 3603 → RELEVANT (Low).** ONDCP primarily channels funding through other agencies (SAMHSA, DOJ), but its own administrative requirements can apply to direct ONDCP-funded programs involving pain management and substance abuse treatment practices.

**Part 176 (ARRA) → RELEVANT (Low).** While ARRA was enacted in 2009, some long-term ARRA-funded programs and subrecipient relationships may still be active. The award terms remain legally operative for any remaining activity. Marginal but included per false-negative avoidance.
