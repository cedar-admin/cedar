# Part-level allowlists for five Tier 3 CFR titles in Cedar's regulatory scope

Cedar's ~99K unclassified eCFR entities reduce sharply at part-level granularity. Across these five Tier 3 titles, **38 parts** (plus 21 TRICARE sections) carry regulatory relevance for Florida independent medical practices — roughly 8% of the total parts in scope. Title 40's hazardous waste pharmaceutical rules (Part 266 Subpart P), Title 47's TCPA implementation (Part 64 §64.1200), Title 28's ADA Title III (Part 36), Title 32's TRICARE provider provisions (Part 199), and Title 38's MISSION Act community care program (Part 17 §§17.4000–17.4040) represent the highest-impact regulatory content. Every remaining part across these five titles is confirmed irrelevant and should be excluded from Cedar's monitoring pipeline.

---

## Title 40: Environmental waste rules that bind every healthcare generator

Title 40 contains **11 relevant parts** concentrated in Subchapters I (Solid Wastes) and J (Superfund/EPCRA). The critical distinction for Cedar: medical practices are waste *generators*, not treatment/storage/disposal facilities (TSDFs) or transporters. Parts governing TSDFs (264, 265, 267, 270) and transporters (263) are excluded.

## Title 47: TCPA dominates an otherwise irrelevant telecommunications code

Title 47 yields only **3 relevant parts** out of hundreds. **Part 64 §64.1200 (TCPA implementation) is the single highest-impact provision** — it governs every patient-facing communication channel (calls, texts, voicemail) for all 14 practice types. The prior AMBIGUOUS classification of Subchapter A Parts 6–7 is **resolved as IRRELEVANT**: these accessibility obligations apply to telecom equipment manufacturers and service providers, not to medical practices as end-users.

## Title 32: TRICARE Part 199 is the sole relevant content

Title 32 spans the entire Department of Defense and intelligence community. For Cedar, only **Part 199 (TRICARE/CHAMPUS)** matters. Florida's concentration of military installations — MacDill AFB/CENTCOM, NAS Jacksonville, NAS Pensacola, Eglin AFB, Patrick SFB, Homestead ARB — creates a significant TRICARE beneficiary population for independent practices.

**Important structural note:** Part 199 does not use formal "Subpart A/B/C" designations. It is organized as individual sections (§§199.1–199.26 plus Appendix A). The table below groups them thematically.

## Conclusion

Three corrections and resolutions from this drill-down merit immediate attention in Cedar's entity classification pipeline. First, **Title 47 Parts 6–7 are definitively IRRELEVANT** — their disability accessibility mandates bind telecom manufacturers and service providers, not medical practice end-users, closing the prior ambiguity. Second, **Title 38 Part 2 is not the privacy rule**: the 38 U.S.C. §7332 supplemental privacy protections (drug abuse, alcoholism, HIV, sickle cell records — more restrictive than HIPAA) reside in **Part 1, §§1.460–1.499**. Cedar's entity router should reclassify accordingly. Third, **Title 40's generator-vs-TSDF distinction** eliminates Parts 263–265, 267, and 270 from scope despite their presence in the "relevant" Subchapter I, cutting nearly half the parts in that range.

The **sewer ban (40 CFR §266.505)** deserves special flagging as the single most universally applicable environmental provision — it binds every healthcare facility in Florida regardless of generator category or Subpart P opt-in status. Similarly, **47 CFR §64.1200 (TCPA)** touches every patient interaction channel and carries the sharpest enforcement risk of any provision in these five titles, particularly given Florida's more restrictive mini-TCPA overlay. For TRICARE (Title 32) and VA Community Care (Title 38), Florida's dense military installation footprint makes these programs operationally significant rather than edge-case, and Cedar should treat them as core monitoring priorities for all 14 practice types.
